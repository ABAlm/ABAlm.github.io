NASA JPL Mars Hard Lander – Electrical System

Capstone Project – Power & PCB Subsystem

This capstone project, sponsored by NASA’s Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL), focuses on designing a Mars hard lander electrical system that can be deployed on the Martian surface to collect environmental data. The system must withstand high-G impact, operate autonomously for multiple sols, and reliably collect and transmit environmental data.

My primary responsibilities are centered on the electrical system: designing the power subsystem, building the Li-ion battery pack with BMS protection, and creating the PCB for the antenna/RF circuit.

System Architecture

The Mars hard-lander node combines sensing, processing, power management, and RF communication into a compact, impact-survivable electrical system. The high-level architecture includes:

  • STM32-based processing and control
  • Environmental and seismic sensors
  • Custom 3S3P Li-ion power subsystem with BMS
  • Multi-rail power conversion (12 V, 5 V, 3.3 V)
  • Antenna and RF front-end PCB for communication

The electrical system must support mission requirements such as multi-sol operation, thermal constraints, and limited available energy while maintaining system reliability.

My Role & Responsibilities

Power Subsystem Lead

Designed the 3S3P Li-ion battery configuration and integrated a BMS to manage protection, balancing, and safe charge/discharge behavior.

Developed and refined the power budget to support multi-sol operation. Created the AX5043 antenna/RF PCB design and schematic using Altium Designer.

PCB Designer

Created the PCB layout for the antenna/RF front-end and contributed to power distribution and decoupling strategies using Altium Designer.

Ensured proper routing, grounding, and interfaces to the STM32 and RF components.

Power Conversion & Budgeting

Selected and analyzed buck converters to derive 5 V and 3.3 V rails from a 12 V battery bus.

Modeled current draw and duty cycles across operating modes to verify that the system meets energy and thermal constraints.

3S3P Li-ion Battery & BMS

The power subsystem is built around a 3S3P Li-ion battery pack designed to provide sufficient energy for multiple sols under expected load conditions. Cells are configured in three series groups, each with three cells in parallel, to balance capacity, voltage, and physical constraints.

  • Assembled and welded the 3S3P pack from individual cells.
  • Integrated a BMS to handle over-voltage, under-voltage, over-current, and cell balancing.
  • Designed connections for safe charging, discharging, and measurement.
  • Verified pack behavior under expected load currents and duty cycles.

Antenna & RF PCB Design

I designed the antenna/RF PCB that interfaces the transceiver to the external antenna and supports proper impedance matching and signal integrity. The layout incorporates ground planes, careful trace routing, and appropriate decoupling.

  • Schematic capture and symbol/footprint management in Altium Designer.
  • Placement and routing of RF components, matching network, and connectors.
  • Used ground pours, vias, and controlled trace paths to minimize noise.
  • Designed to integrate cleanly with the STM32-based main board.

Power Budget & Multi-Sol Operation

Using estimated and measured current draw from each subsystem, I helped construct the power budget to verify that the node can operate for the required number of sols. This included:

  • Modeling average and peak current for sensors, MCU, RF, and housekeeping loads.
  • Estimating duty cycles for each mode (sleep, sense, transmit, etc.).
  • Checking capacity vs. consumption curves against the 3S3P configuration.
  • Identifying high-risk scenarios where load or temperature could threaten runtime.

Testing & Validation

I contributed to early proof-of-concept testing for the power and PCB subsystems to reduce risk going into later phases of the project:

  • Battery discharge tests using INA219 current and voltage logging.
  • Verification of BMS cutoff thresholds and recovery behavior.
